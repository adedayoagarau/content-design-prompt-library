## Speaking Content Architecture & Stage Presence System v1.0

**Purpose**: Design a comprehensive executive speaking framework that transforms executives into compelling speakers through strategic talk development, stage presence mastery, audience engagement techniques, and systematic content repurposing that amplifies business impact far beyond the event itself.

**Context**: Use this framework when building or refining an executive's speaking capability—from first-time speakers to experienced presenters optimizing their impact. Ideal for executives who need to keynote industry conferences, present at customer events, pitch to investors, address employees at all-hands, or represent their company on major stages. Applicable across industries where speaking is a critical executive competency and business development tool.

**Strategic Frameworks Applied**:
- **Narrative Architecture** - Story-driven talk structures that resonate emotionally
- **Stage Presence Development** - Voice, body language, and delivery optimization
- **Audience Psychology** - Understanding and influencing listener behavior
- **Content Repurposing Engine** - Maximizing ROI from each talk across channels
- **Speaking Circuit Strategy** - Progressive opportunity cultivation and tier advancement
- **Business Impact Attribution** - Connecting speaking to measurable outcomes

## Prompt

You are a strategic speaking architect designing a comprehensive keynote and presentation system for **{{executive_name}}** of **{{company_name}}**. Your task is to develop **{{signature_talks}}** that establish authority in **{{expertise_areas}}**, engage **{{target_audiences}}** across **{{event_tiers}}**, and drive **{{speaking_goals}}** through compelling narrative structure, authentic **{{natural_style}}** delivery, strategic content repurposing, and systematic business impact attribution.

### Signature Talk Development

**Core Talk Architecture:**

Every executive needs 2-4 signature talks that can be delivered at different lengths and adapted for various audiences:

**Talk 1 - Flagship Keynote (Primary Thought Leadership):**
- **Title**: Compelling, provocative, or curiosity-generating (not generic)
  - Weak: "The Future of [Industry]"
  - Strong: "Why [Industry] Is Solving the Wrong Problem (And What to Do Instead)"
- **Core Thesis**: Single, bold, defensible argument
- **Target Audience**: **{{target_audiences}}** primary segment
- **Talk Length Variations**:
  - 5-minute version (elevator pitch, podcast intro)
  - 20-minute version (standard conference slot)
  - 45-minute version (keynote with Q&A)
  - 90-minute version (workshop or masterclass)
- **Modular Structure**: Can extract sections for different contexts

**Narrative Framework - The Hero's Journey for Business Talks:**

```
Act 1 - Setup (20% of talk):
├─ Hook (0-2 min): Pattern interrupt that demands attention
│  ├─ Surprising statistic
│  ├─ Provocative question
│  ├─ Personal anecdote
│  └─ Counterintuitive statement
├─ Problem Introduction (2-5 min): Why audience should care
│  ├─ Industry challenge
│  ├─ Audience pain point
│  └─ Status quo limitations
└─ Credibility Establishment (5-8 min): Why you're qualified to speak
   ├─ Personal story or journey
   ├─ Relevant experience
   └─ Proprietary insights/data

Act 2 - Conflict & Resolution (60% of talk):
├─ Journey (8-25 min): How you/your company confronted problem
│  ├─ Obstacles faced
│  ├─ Failures and pivots
│  ├─ Breakthrough moments
│  └─ Lessons learned
├─ Framework/Solution (25-35 min): Actionable approach
│  ├─ 3-5 key principles (rule of threes)
│  ├─ Supporting evidence/examples
│  ├─ Data validation
│  └─ Real-world application
└─ Case Studies (35-50 min): Proof it works
   ├─ Customer success stories
   ├─ Industry examples
   └─ Measurable outcomes

Act 3 - Resolution (20% of talk):
├─ Future Vision (50-55 min): Where this leads
│  ├─ Industry implications
│  ├─ Opportunities ahead
│  └─ Challenges remaining
├─ Audience Application (55-60 min): What they should do
│  ├─ Immediate actions
│  ├─ Long-term strategy
│  └─ Resource access
└─ Call to Action (60+ min): Clear next step
   ├─ Connect with speaker
   ├─ Try framework/tool
   ├─ Join movement/community
   └─ Q&A transition
```

**Hook Strategies (Critical First 2 Minutes):**

**Option 1 - The Surprising Stat:**
```
"Last year, [industry] spent $[X] billion on [solution].
95% of it was wasted. Here's why—and what to do instead."
```

**Option 2 - The Personal Failure:**
```
"Five years ago, I made a decision that cost my company $10 million
and nearly destroyed everything we'd built. It was also the best thing
that ever happened to us."
```

**Option 3 - The Provocative Question:**
```
"Raise your hand if you think [conventional wisdom] is true.
[Pause for hands]
I'm going to spend the next 30 minutes proving you're wrong."
```

**Option 4 - The Counterintuitive Statement:**
```
"Everything you know about [topic] is backwards.
The companies winning right now are doing the exact opposite
of what industry experts recommend."
```

**Option 5 - The Relatable Story:**
```
"Three months into my first CEO role, I had to lay off 30% of my team.
I was 32 years old, had never fired anyone, and was about to face
60 people who trusted me. This is what I learned..."
```

**Content Development Process:**

**1. Core Message Clarity:**
- What is the single idea you want audience to remember?
- Can you state it in one sentence?
- Is it specific enough to be actionable but broad enough to matter?

**2. Audience Analysis:**
- What does **{{target_audiences}}** care about most?
- What keeps them up at night?
- What would make them pull out phones to take notes?
- What would make them share on social media?

**3. Proof Points Collection:**
- Personal stories (15-20 anecdotes across career)
- Company data and case studies
- Customer testimonials and outcomes
- Industry research and third-party validation
- Proprietary insights from **{{expertise_areas}}**

**4. Three-Point Structure:**
- Every talk needs 3 main sections (brain remembers threes)
- Each section needs 3 supporting points
- Each point needs 1-2 stories or examples
- Total: 9-18 stories in your rotation

**5. Modular Design:**
- Each section can stand alone
- Reorder for different audiences
- Extract for different time lengths
- Repurpose for written content

**Talk 2 - Industry/Technical Deep Dive:**
- More specialized than flagship keynote
- For **{{target_audiences}}** who are practitioners
- Heavy on frameworks, data, actionable tactics
- Positions as domain expert vs. inspirational speaker

**Talk 3 - Personal Journey/Leadership:**
- Origin story and lessons learned
- Broader audience appeal (not just industry insiders)
- Emotional connection and relatability
- Good for all-hands, university talks, general business events

**Talk 4 - Hot Topic/Trend Commentary:**
- Reactive to current industry developments
- Demonstrates thought leadership and relevance
- Shorter lifecycle (6-12 months)
- Good for media appearances and rapid-response opportunities

### Stage Presence & Delivery Mastery

**Voice & Vocal Techniques:**

**Pace & Rhythm:**
- **Conversational pace**: 150-160 words per minute (vs. rushed 180+)
- **Strategic pauses**: After key points (3-5 seconds feels eternal but creates impact)
- **Vocal variety**: Vary speed, volume, pitch to maintain engagement
- **Emphasis**: Slow down and louder for key points, not faster and louder

**Projection & Clarity:**
- Speak to back of room (even with microphone)
- Articulate clearly (especially technical terms)
- Drop register for authority (deeper voice communicates confidence)
- Avoid uptalk (statements shouldn't sound like questions)

**Emotional Modulation:**
- Match tone to content (serious for challenges, energized for solutions)
- Smile for positive content (audience can hear smiling)
- Authenticity over perfection (controlled vulnerability resonates)

**Body Language & Stage Craft:**

**Posture & Movement:**
- Open stance (avoid crossed arms, hands in pockets)
- Root in power pose before starting (confidence anchoring)
- Move with purpose (not pacing nervously)
- Use stage geography: Center for main points, sides for stories/asides

**Gestures:**
- Natural gestures from shoulders (not just wrists)
- Match gesture size to room (bigger room = bigger gestures)
- Count on fingers for numbered points (visual reinforcement)
- Hands visible (not hidden behind podium or in pockets)

**Eye Contact:**
- Scan entire room (not just front row or slides)
- Hold eye contact 3-5 seconds per person (creates connection)
- Virtual presentations: Look at camera, not screen

**Slides & Visual Design:**

**Slide Philosophy:**
- Slides support speaker, not replace speaker
- Audience should listen to you, not read slides
- One idea per slide maximum
- More slides with less content > fewer slides packed with text

**Visual Hierarchy:**
- **Title slides**: Clean, minimal text
- **Content slides**: One headline + one image or one stat
- **Data visualization**: Simple charts, one insight highlighted
- **Quote slides**: Large, readable text (audience members should be able to photograph and read on phone)
- **Transition slides**: Section breaks with visual distinction

**Design Standards:**
- Consistent brand template (fonts, colors, layout)
- High-resolution images (no pixelation on large screens)
- Readable from back of room (minimum 30pt font)
- Contrast for readability (dark on light or light on dark, no gray on gray)
- Minimal animations (only when they add understanding)

**Slide-Free Zones:**
- Personal stories (black screen or static image, focus on speaker)
- Q&A (slides off, direct conversation)
- Emotional moments (remove visual distraction)

**Handling Nervousness:**

Based on **{{nervousness_level}}**:

**Pre-Talk Rituals:**
- Physical warm-up (jumping jacks, arm swings—release tension)
- Vocal warm-up (tongue twisters, humming, projecting)
- Power posing (2 minutes in private before going on)
- Visualization (see yourself succeeding, audience engaged)
- Breathing exercises (box breathing: 4-4-4-4)

**On-Stage Techniques:**
- Start with practiced opening (muscle memory takes over)
- Find friendly faces in audience (nod and smile back)
- If mind blanks: Pause, breathe, "Let me say that another way"
- Acknowledge butterflies if extreme: "I'm excited to share this" (reframe anxiety)
- Remember: Audience wants you to succeed

**Post-Talk Recovery:**
- Adrenaline crash is normal
- Don't over-analyze immediately after
- Save energy for networking (event impact happens after talk)

### Talk Preparation & Rehearsal

**Development Timeline:**

**For New Talk (8-12 weeks):**
- **Week 1-2**: Core message development, outline, audience analysis
- **Week 3-4**: Story collection, proof points, draft narrative
- **Week 5-6**: Slide design, visual development
- **Week 7-8**: First rehearsal, feedback, revisions
- **Week 9-10**: Polish rehearsal, timing, transitions
- **Week 11-12**: Final rehearsal, Q&A preparation, contingency planning

**For Existing Talk (1-2 weeks):**
- Audience customization (examples, references)
- Timing adjustment (if different length)
- Slide updates (data refresh, new examples)
- Run-through rehearsal (1-2 times)
- Q&A prep for this specific audience

**Rehearsal Strategy:**

**Solo Rehearsal:**
- Out loud full run-throughs (not just reading slides)
- Record video for self-review (painful but essential)
- Time each section (identify what runs long)
- Practice transitions (where talks often stumble)

**Feedback Rehearsal:**
- Present to trusted colleagues (**{{support_resources}}**)
- Specific feedback requests: "Is core message clear?" "Do stories land?" "Where did you lose interest?"
- Run full Q&A practice (anticipate tough questions)

**Dress Rehearsal:**
- In similar environment (standing, using remote, big screen)
- All tech tested (slide advance, videos play, microphone)
- Wearing similar outfit (check comfort, pockets for remote)
- Simulate Q&A and timing pressure

**Q&A Preparation:**
- Anticipate 10-15 likely questions
- Prepare concise answers (60-90 seconds each)
- Have stories ready for common questions
- Practice "I don't know, but I'll find out" (honesty when stumped)
- Redirect off-topic questions: "Great question, but outside today's scope—happy to discuss after"

### Event Strategy & Opportunity Cultivation

**Speaking Opportunity Tiers:**

**Tier 1 - Flagship Events (Target: 2-4/year):**
- Major industry conferences (10,000+ attendees)
- Keynote slots (not panels)
- High production value, recorded, widely distributed
- Speaking fee offered (typically $10K-$50K+ for established speakers)
- Examples: TED/TEDx, Web Summit, Dreamforce, Grace Hopper, Collision

**Tier 2 - Strategic Events (Target: 6-12/year):**
- Industry conferences (1,000-10,000 attendees)
- Keynotes or premium speaking slots
- **{{target_audiences}}** heavily represented
- May offer speaking fee or comp travel
- Examples: Regional tech conferences, industry association summits

**Tier 3 - Growth Events (Target: 12-24/year):**
- Local or emerging conferences (100-1,000 attendees)
- Standard speaking slots
- Build experience and content
- Typically no fee (exposure opportunity)
- Examples: Meetups, university talks, webinars, company events

**Speaking Fee Structure:**

As authority builds:
- **Starting out**: No fee (building portfolio)
- **Emerging speaker**: $2,500-$5,000 + travel
- **Established speaker**: $10,000-$25,000
- **Recognized authority**: $25,000-$75,000+
- **Celebrity speaker**: $100,000+

Considerations:
- Waive fee for strategic opportunities (flagship events, key customer/partner)
- Charge for pure speaking bureaus (standard for professional circuit)
- Balance paid vs. unpaid to maintain strategic optionality

**Opportunity Evaluation Framework:**

For each speaking request, assess:

```yaml
opportunity_score:
  audience_fit:
    question: "Is {{target_audiences}} in attendance?"
    score: "High (3) | Medium (2) | Low (1)"

  business_alignment:
    question: "Does this support {{speaking_goals}}?"
    score: "Direct (3) | Indirect (2) | Neutral (1)"

  brand_elevation:
    question: "Does this elevate or maintain brand positioning?"
    score: "Elevates (3) | Maintains (2) | Dilutes (1)"

  time_investment:
    question: "ROI given travel and prep time?"
    score: "High ROI (3) | Break-even (2) | Low ROI (1)"

  recording_distribution:
    question: "Will talk be recorded and widely distributed?"
    score: "Yes, wide (3) | Yes, limited (2) | No (1)"

  total_score:
    decision: "12-15 = Must do | 8-11 = Strong consider | <8 = Decline unless strategic"
```

**Event Preparation Checklist:**

**2 Weeks Before:**
- Confirm talk title, length, audience size/profile
- Request AV specs (screen size, aspect ratio, confidence monitor, remote type)
- Ask about recording (will it be recorded? Can you access? Can you distribute?)
- Submit bio and headshot
- Customize talk for this specific audience

**1 Week Before:**
- Final rehearsal
- Prep Q&A for this audience
- Coordinate logistics (flights, hotel, ground transport)
- Pack backup (slides on USB, laptop, remote, adapters)

**Day Before:**
- Tech check if possible (test slides, advance, video playback)
- Walk the stage if accessible
- Prep outfit (comfortable, professional, appropriate for audience and venue)
- Early night (rest matters more than last-minute tweaks)

**Day Of:**
- Arrive 60+ minutes early
- Full tech check (slides, clicker, microphone, confidence monitor)
- Meet organizer and A/V team
- Review timing and cues
- Physical and vocal warm-up
- Bathroom, water, breath mints
- Phone on airplane mode (in pocket or offstage)

**Post-Talk:**
- Stay for Q&A and networking (where business development happens)
- Collect business cards from key connections
- Take photo of audience (if allowed) for social media
- Request recording and permission to distribute
- Send thank-you to organizer within 24 hours

### Content Repurposing & Amplification

**Maximizing ROI from Each Talk:**

One 45-minute keynote can generate 20+ content assets:

**Before Event:**
1. **Social media tease** (LinkedIn/Twitter announcement)
2. **Blog post preview** of key ideas
3. **Email to list** inviting local attendees
4. **Slide deck** shared on SlideShare/LinkedIn

**During Event:**
5. **Live tweeting** key quotes (by team or attendees)
6. **Instagram stories** backstage and on-stage
7. **Audience photos** (engaged crowd, standing ovation)

**After Event:**
8. **Full recording** (YouTube, gated on website)
9. **Highlight reel** (90-second sizzle of best moments)
10. **Quote graphics** (5-10 shareable images from talk)
11. **LinkedIn article** (written version of talk)
12. **Blog series** (each main section becomes post)
13. **Podcast episode** (audio from talk or discussion of themes)
14. **Email newsletter** sharing recording and key takeaways
15. **Slide deck republish** with speaker notes added
16. **Medium/Substack post** for different audience
17. **Twitter thread** summarizing key points
18. **Audiograms** (short clips with captions for social)
19. **Case study** (if talk led to business outcomes)
20. **Press release** (if major event or announcement)

**Distribution Strategy:**

**Owned Channels:**
- Website (speaking page with recordings and slides)
- YouTube channel (organize by talk themes)
- Email list (share with subscribers)
- Social media (LinkedIn, Twitter, Instagram as appropriate)

**Earned Channels:**
- Event organizer sharing (tag and thank them)
- Attendee social posts (monitor and reshare)
- Media coverage of event
- Speaker bureau promotion (if applicable)

**Paid Channels:**
- Promote video on LinkedIn/YouTube (boost best-performing content)
- Sponsored content featuring talk themes
- Conference promotional channels (if they offer)

**Long-Term Content Library:**

Organize all speaking content:
- **Signature Talks**: Current versions with timestamps
- **Story Bank**: All anecdotes cataloged by theme
- **Slide Library**: Reusable slides, charts, graphics organized
- **Video Archive**: All recordings with metadata (event, date, audience)
- **Press Mentions**: Any media coverage from talks
- **Testimonials**: Audience feedback and quotes

This library becomes asset for:
- Speaking bureaus (sizzle reel and samples)
- Future talk development (repurpose proven content)
- Content marketing (ongoing social and blog material)
- Book proposal (if pursuing)

## Examples

### Example 1: First-Time CEO Speaker - SaaS Startup

**Context**: First-time CEO, Series B SaaS company, minimal speaking experience, significant stage anxiety, needs to keynote flagship customer conference in 3 months.

**Approach**:
- **Talk Development**: 12-week timeline, focused on company origin story and product vision
- **Title**: "Why We Built [Product] (And Why Traditional [Category] Is Broken)"
- **Structure**: 70% personal journey, 30% product/industry insights (leverage authentic founder story)
- **Slide Design**: Minimal text, customer success photos, simple product demos
- **Preparation**: Weekly coaching sessions, recorded rehearsals, practicing in front of team
- **Nervousness**: Power posing before talk, memorized first 2 minutes, deep breathing between sections

**Delivery**:
- 30-minute keynote for 800 customers
- Authentic, slightly nervous delivery actually enhanced relatability
- Led with personal failure story (vulnerability built connection)
- Ended with customer success montage (emotional high point)

**Results**:
- Standing ovation (surprising for first major talk)
- 45 customer business cards collected post-talk
- 12 upsell conversations initiated within 30 days ($2.3M pipeline)
- Recording viewed 5,000+ times (internal sales team uses in pitches)
- Invited to speak at 3 industry conferences based on performance
- CEO confidence transformed—now seeks speaking opportunities

**Key Learning**: Authenticity and vulnerability beat polish for first-time speakers. Customers connected with genuine founder story more than they would have with slick corporate presentation.

### Example 2: Technical CTO - AI Conference Circuit

**Context**: CTO of AI infrastructure company, deep technical expertise, naturally data-driven speaker, building authority on conference circuit to support Series C fundraising.

**Approach**:

- **Proof Points**: Proprietary data from 200+ customer deployments, quantified cost savings
- **Slide Design**: Heavy on data visualizations, code snippets, architecture diagrams
- **Target**: Technical conferences (NeurIPS, MLSys, PyTorch Conference)

**Speaking Strategy**:

- **Year 2**: 12 conferences including 2 Tier 1 keynotes, launched podcast interviewing AI researchers
- **Content Repurposing**: Each talk became blog post, Twitter thread, YouTube video

**Results (24 months)**:
- Recognized as thought leader in AI infrastructure space
- Invited to keynote NeurIPS (10,000+ attendees, dream event for technical audience)

- Now charges $15K speaking fee for commercial events (donates to open-source projects)

**Key Learning**: Technical speakers should lean into data and depth vs. trying to be inspirational. Authentic technical expertise resonates with technical audiences.

## Best Practices

- **Start with Story**: Personal narratives beat data dumps for audience engagement
- **Simplify Relentlessly**: One big idea per talk, three supporting points
- **Rehearse Out Loud**: Reading slides ≠ delivering talk; practice speaking
- **Design for Back Row**: Slides readable from worst seat, voice projects to entire room
- **Front-Load Value**: First 5 minutes determine if audience stays engaged
- **Modular Content**: Build talks from reusable sections for different contexts
- **Record Everything**: Every talk is content asset—get video when possible
- **Network After**: Business development happens post-talk, not during
- **Repurpose Systematically**: One talk = 20+ content pieces
- **Measure Business Outcomes**: Track pipeline, not just applause
- **Continuous Improvement**: Review recordings, get feedback, iterate
- **Selective Saying Yes**: Quality events > quantity of events
- **Authentic > Perfect**: Genuine connection beats polished corporate speak
- **Prepare for Q&A**: Anticipate questions, practice concise answers

## Common Pitfalls

- **Slide Reading**: Slides should support, not replace speaker
- **Data Overload**: Too many statistics without narrative thread
- **No Clear Thesis**: Meandering talk without single core message
- **Weak Opening**: Generic introduction that doesn't hook audience
- **No Rehearsal**: "Winging it" leads to poor timing, missed points, nervousness
- **Ignoring Audience**: Not customizing talk for specific attendee interests
- **Selling Too Hard**: Pitch disguised as thought leadership alienates audience
- **No Repurposing**: Speaking without capturing content for broader distribution
- **Poor Time Management**: Running long and getting cut off mid-talk
- **All Yes, No Strategy**: Speaking at every opportunity without ROI evaluation
- **Copying Others**: Mimicking famous speakers vs. finding authentic voice
- **Static Content**: Giving same exact talk for years without updates
- **No Business Attribution**: Speaking without tracking business outcomes
- **Perfectionism**: Waiting until talk is perfect before accepting opportunities (perfect comes from doing)
- **Neglecting Q&A**: Unprepared for questions, fumbling answers
